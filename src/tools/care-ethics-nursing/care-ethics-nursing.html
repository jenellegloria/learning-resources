An Ethic
of Caring

Most of us enter health professions because we care. But Nel Noddings argues that genuine care is harder and more specific than good intentions. It requires a particular kind of presence — one that professional training, institutional protocols, and the sheer weight of a busy shift can quietly erode.

You'll move through four clinical scenarios — each placing you in a different health professions role, each foregrounding a different concept from Noddings' framework. Before each scenario, you'll learn the concept. Then you'll try to live it in a moment that doesn't make it easy.

This exercise has no right answers. Care ethics is particular — what matters is the quality of your attention to the specific person in front of you.

Care in the clinic

Noddings did not write specifically for health professionals — but her framework speaks directly to clinical life. The relationship between nurse and patient, provider and family, is exactly the kind of encounter she has in mind: one person in need, another with the capacity to respond.

What threatens genuine care in clinical settings is not bad intentions. It is efficiency, protocol, and the sheer cognitive load of the shift. These pressures push us toward managing patients rather than meeting them. Noddings calls this treating the other as a category rather than a person.

In each scenario, you will face exactly this pressure. The question is whether you can find your way back to the particular person — their reality, their need, their moment — despite it.

Scenario 1 · The Patient
Engrossment

When Noddings describes engrossment, she means fully receiving the other's reality — not standing outside someone's experience and deciding how to respond to it, but actually inhabiting it with them. The one-caring, she writes, is "present in her own acts" and attends to the cared-for without self-interest eclipsing what she sees.

In clinical settings, engrossment is constantly threatened. Efficiency pressures, cognitive load, the next patient — all of these pull you toward managing the person in front of you rather than truly meeting them. Noddings would say that the moment you stop seeing a patient as this specific person and start seeing them as a task to complete, care has already been lost.

Scenario 1 · The Patient
Being
received

"We are engrossed in the other. We have received him and feel his pain or happiness."

— Nel Noddings, Caring (p.83)

Watch for this in yourself

When you enter Mr. Okafor's room, notice your first impulse. Is it to complete a task and leave? To reassure him efficiently? Noddings asks something harder: can you actually receive what this person is communicating — not just the stated request, but what the repeated call button might be telling you about his experience right now?

Scenario 2 · The Night Shift
The
"I Must"

Noddings describes the "I must" as a moral feeling — an inner voice that arises in response to another person's need. It is not a rule. It precedes reasoning. It is the felt pull of genuine care before you have decided what to do.

But she is honest: this feeling doesn't always come. Sometimes it "whispers faintly and disappears, leaving distrust, repugnance, or hate." When natural caring fails — when you're tired, frustrated, or dealing with someone you find difficult — ethical caring is the effort to act as one-caring anyway. Not because a rule says so, but because you recognize the value of the caring relation itself.

Scenario 2 · The Night Shift
Natural vs.
Ethical Caring

"This memory of our own best moments of caring and being cared for sweeps over us as a feeling — as an 'I must' — in response to the plight of the other."

— Nel Noddings, Caring (p.79)

Watch for this in yourself

Before you enter Mrs. Delacroix's room at 3am, notice what you feel. Noddings doesn't ask you to pretend the tiredness or irritation isn't there. She asks: can you, despite it, choose to meet her as one-caring? Can you reach for the memory of what it feels like to be in pain and unheard — and let that guide you in?

Scenario 3 · The Home Visit
Motivational
Displacement

Motivational displacement is Noddings' term for what happens when one-caring temporarily sets aside their own agenda so the cared-for's needs can guide the response. It doesn't mean losing yourself — it means making room for the other person's reality to actually reach you.

In a community health context, this is especially challenging. You have a care plan. You have outcomes to achieve. You have more patients after this one. The temptation is to lead with what you need from the patient rather than first receiving what the patient is actually communicating. Noddings would call that a failure of care, even if the clinical content is correct.

Scenario 4 · The Family
The Critique
of Principles

Noddings argues that principle-based ethics — rules like "follow protocol," "defer to the physician," "stay in your scope" — require us to abstract away from the particular situation. To apply a principle, you must treat this encounter as an instance of a category. And in doing so, you often lose the very features that make the moral question real.

This does not mean protocols are useless. But Noddings insists: "before I decide, I must turn away from this analytic chain of thought and back to the concrete situation. Here is this person with this perceived need to which is attached this importance." The principle can inform — it cannot replace attending to what is actually in front of you.

Scenario 4 · The Family
The concrete
situation

"I must put justification aside temporarily. Here is this person with this perceived need."

— Nel Noddings, Caring (p.84)

Watch for this in yourself

The protocol exists for real reasons. But notice: when the Nguyen daughter asks "Is he dying?", what does following the protocol do to your relationship with this family — and to them? Noddings asks you to hold both: the legitimate institutional constraint and the particular human need in front of you. What does care require here?

Scenario 4 · The Family
Principles
abstract away

Noddings writes that in order to apply a moral principle universally, "we should have to establish that human predicaments exhibit sufficient sameness — and this we cannot do without abstracting away from concrete situations those qualities that seem to reveal the sameness. In doing this, we often lose the very qualities or factors that gave rise to the moral question."

The Nguyen family is not a category. They are these people, in this moment, after three admissions, asking this question. The protocol was not written for them. It was written for cases in the abstract. Noddings asks: what does caring require when you actually see who is in front of you?
