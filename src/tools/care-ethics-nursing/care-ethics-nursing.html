An Ethic
of Caring

Most of us probably have an intuitive sense of what it means to "care" about someone. Nel Noddings' theory takes that gut feeling seriously — she sees it as the very foundation of morality. But genuinely caring, the way she thinks morality requires us to do, isn't always easy.

Caring professions place enormous demands on our time and attention. The more we're pulled in different directions, the harder it becomes to be truly present with the person in front of us. For Noddings, staying present isn't just a nice quality to have — it's the whole point of what we do, and a skill we have to keep building throughout our careers (and our lives).

In this activity, you are going to be presented with four imaginary but realistic scenarios a healthcare worker might encounter. Before each scenario, we'll review one concept from Noddings' theory. The scenario will help you apply that concept and reflect on what it really means in practice.

There are no right answers here. What matters is paying attention to the specific person in front of you.

A note before you begin

Noddings didn't write specifically for healthcare — but her ideas fit clinical life really well. At the heart of her theory is a simple but demanding idea: genuine care means being fully present to the specific person in front of you, not just going through the motions of helping.

That's harder than it sounds in a clinical setting. Time pressure, protocols, and the weight of everything else on your plate can push you toward getting through tasks rather than truly connecting. Noddings would say that when that happens, something important gets lost — even if the clinical work is technically correct.

Each scenario in this activity puts you in exactly that kind of moment. See if you can find your way to the person — not just the task.

Scenario 1 · The Patient
Being fully
present

Noddings uses the word engrossment to describe something we've probably all experienced — that feeling of being completely absorbed in another person's reality. Not just listening, but actually letting what they're going through land on you.

Think about a time someone was truly present with you when you were struggling. They weren't distracted, they weren't already thinking about what to say next — they were just there. For Noddings, that quality of presence isn't just a nice personality trait. It's what genuine care actually requires.

In a busy clinical setting, this is harder than it sounds. There's always something pulling your attention away. Noddings would say that the moment you start seeing a patient as a task to complete rather than a person to meet, something essential has already been lost.

Scenario 1 · The Patient
What this
looks like

"We are engrossed in the other. We have received him and feel his pain or happiness."

— Nel Noddings, Caring (p.83)

Something to notice in yourself

When you walk into Mr. Okafor's room, pay attention to your first instinct. Is it to check the task off and move on? To give a quick reassurance? Those impulses are understandable — but Noddings is asking something different: can you actually take in what this person might be communicating, beyond the surface request? What might pressing that call button four times really be about?

Scenario 2 · The Night Shift
When caring
doesn't come easily

Sometimes caring feels natural — you genuinely want to help and being present comes easily. Noddings calls this natural caring. But what about when it doesn't? When you're exhausted, or the patient is difficult, or you've already been called three times tonight?

Noddings is honest about this. She acknowledges that the impulse to care can "whisper faintly and disappear" — especially when we're stressed or dealing with someone we find frustrating. She calls the effort to care anyway ethical caring: choosing to show up as a caring person even when it doesn't come naturally.

This isn't about faking it. It's about remembering why you're there — and choosing, consciously, to meet this person as a person.

Scenario 2 · The Night Shift
The feeling
of "I must"

"This memory of our own best moments of caring and being cared for sweeps over us as a feeling — as an 'I must' — in response to the plight of the other."

— Nel Noddings, Caring (p.79)

Something to notice in yourself

Before you go into Mrs. Delacroix's room, check in with yourself. What are you feeling? Noddings isn't asking you to pretend the tiredness or frustration isn't real. She's asking whether you can — despite it — call up the memory of what it feels like to be in pain and not be heard. Can you let that feeling guide you through the door?

Scenario 3 · The Home Visit
Motivational
Displacement

Motivational displacement is Noddings' term for what happens when one-caring temporarily sets aside their own agenda so the cared-for's needs can guide the response. It doesn't mean losing yourself — it means making room for the other person's reality to actually reach you.

In a community health context, this is especially challenging. You have a care plan. You have outcomes to achieve. You have more patients after this one. The temptation is to lead with what you need from the patient rather than first receiving what the patient is actually communicating. Noddings would call that a failure of care, even if the clinical content is correct.

Scenario 3 · The Home Visit

Care-led response
"He said 'I know what you're going to say.' Something has shifted. What is he actually telling me right now?"

Watch for this in yourself

When Daniel says "I know what you're going to say," he may be telling you something important about his experience — of the visits, the plan, his own life. Motivational displacement means being willing to set aside what you came to accomplish and actually hear what he's communicating before you respond.

Scenario 4 · The Family
The problem
with rules

Most ethical frameworks are built around rules or principles — "always tell the truth," "do the most good," "follow the protocol." These rules are meant to apply consistently, no matter who is involved. That consistency is part of the point.

But Noddings has a problem with this. She argues that to apply a rule consistently, you have to treat every situation as basically the same — and in doing so, you often end up ignoring the very details that make this situation different, and morally real. The rule handles "the case." It doesn't see the person.

This doesn't mean rules are useless. It means they can't be the whole story. Care ethics asks you to look at the actual person in front of you before you reach for the policy.

Scenario 4 · The Family
Starting with
the person

"I must put justification aside temporarily. Here is this person with this perceived need."

— Nel Noddings, Caring (p.84)

Something to notice in yourself

The protocol is there for real reasons — and you don't have to abandon it. But before you decide what to do, try doing what Noddings suggests: set the rulebook aside for just a moment and look at who is actually in front of you. The Nguyen family isn't a policy scenario. They are these people, in this hallway, asking this question. What does that call for?

Scenario 4 · The Family
What gets lost
in abstraction

Noddings points out that rules and principles are written for situations in general — not for any particular person. To apply them, you have to treat this situation as interchangeable with all the others the rule was designed for. And when you do that, you often lose exactly what made this situation matter.

The Nguyen family isn't a general case. They are these specific people — after three admissions, a sleepless night, a flight from overseas — asking this question, right now, to you. The protocol wasn't written for them. It was written for the abstract. What does being present with them actually require?
